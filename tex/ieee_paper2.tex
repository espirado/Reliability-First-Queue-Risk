\begin{abstract}
Queue-delay predictors for GPU clusters are useful only if their probabilities are reliable: a ``70\% risk'' should occur roughly 70\% of the time. We present a reliability-first, open-source pipeline that treats probability \emph{calibration} as a first-class objective alongside discrimination and latency, and we demonstrate an operational integration path from offline benchmark to production SLOs. The pipeline spans four heterogeneous environments---Amazon EKS (1.2M pod events), AWS ParallelCluster Slurm HPC (555 GPU jobs with DCGM telemetry), the public Alibaba GPU trace, and the public Google Borg trace---evaluates five model families (Logistic Regression, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, XGBoost, LightGBM) with post-hoc isotonic calibration, and assigns each trained model to one of four operational tiers (warn / advisory / suggest / gate) via SLO-compliance tests. On Slurm we observe that enriching submit-time features with aggregated DCGM telemetry raises Logistic Regression from AUROC $0.498$ (tier: \textit{warn}) to $0.815$ (tier: \textit{gate}) while cutting ECE from $0.066$ to $0.027$; Gradient Boosting achieves ECE $0.0018$ and qualifies for admission-gating. On EKS-Dec, all five models qualify for \textit{gate} after isotonic calibration (ECE $\leq 0.041$ with bootstrap 95\% CIs). On Alibaba and Borg, calibration holds (ECE $\leq 0.031$) even when discrimination collapses, which we argue is the right behavior for honest advisory systems. Our cross-cluster analysis shows the Population Stability Index of \texttt{pending\_ratio} between EKS and Slurm reaches $12.4$, motivating per-cluster recalibration rather than global retraining; a temporal-ECE monitor detects drift at window seven of eight and triggers recalibration. To stress-test the calibration-monitoring SLI design beyond the $555$-job real Slurm trace we also ship a \emph{controlled scaling experiment}: a trace-calibrated discrete-event Slurm simulator (in the methodological tradition of BatSim and the Lublin--Feitelson workload model) that runs the full pipeline on $10{,}000$-job workloads under controlled arrival and telemetry perturbations. Under a controlled input-distribution shift the calibration SLI fires as designed---ECE degrades $32$--$36\times$ while AUROC stays at $0.99$---providing a unit-test for the ``monitor calibration, not just discrimination'' design choice. The full pipeline, simulator harness, data samples, trained calibrators, and a single reproducibility notebook are archived on Zenodo so that operators can reproduce every figure in this paper and port the SLO-driven recalibration loop to their own Kubernetes or Slurm clusters.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Submit-time queue-risk prediction has become a popular tool in GPU-cluster operations: an ML model estimates the probability that a newly-submitted job will wait longer than some SLO threshold, and downstream systems advise the user or adjust scheduling. For this workflow to be safe, the probabilities must be \emph{reliable}---a ``70\% risk'' should occur about 70\% of the time. Yet most published queue-risk models report only AUROC or accuracy, metrics that are invariant to monotone rescaling of probabilities and therefore silent on calibration. When an uncalibrated model is wired into an admission webhook, miscalibration becomes user-visible error: the system gates jobs the user believes are safe, or admits jobs the user believes are risky. Trust, once lost, is hard to recover.

This paper describes \textit{reliability-first} queue-risk prediction as a scientific-software contribution. We treat probability calibration, drift detection, SLO compliance, and safe rollout as first-class engineering objectives, and we release the entire toolchain---feature engineering, five model families, isotonic post-hoc calibration, the tier-qualification algorithm, PSI-based drift monitor, and sliding-window recalibrator---as an end-to-end reproducible pipeline with a single Jupyter notebook that regenerates every figure in this paper.

\subsection{Contributions}
\begin{itemize}
    \item A \textbf{reproducible calibration benchmark} across four heterogeneous GPU-cluster environments (EKS, Slurm, Alibaba, Borg) spanning $\sim$1.2M job events and three scheduler families, with bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals and McNemar significance tests on five model families.
    \item An empirical demonstration that \textbf{DCGM telemetry aggregated at submit time} rescues a linear model on Slurm: Logistic Regression with all features achieves ECE $0.027$ and qualifies for admission-\textit{gate}, versus ECE $0.066$ and \textit{warn}-only with submit-time features alone.
    \item A four-tier \textbf{operational qualification algorithm} that maps calibration and tail-behavior tests to concrete operational roles (warn / advisory / suggest / gate), enabling operators to choose risk-appropriate integrations without re-reading the full evaluation.
    \item A \textbf{drift-to-recalibration pipeline} that combines PSI on submit-time features with a temporal-ECE monitor, triggering sliding-window isotonic recalibration before SLO alarms fire.
    \item A \textbf{controlled scaling experiment} (\S\ref{sec:sim}) built on a trace-calibrated discrete-event Slurm simulator, used as a reproducibility harness for the calibration pipeline: it (a) confirms that the bootstrap variance estimates from the $555$-job trace are statistically conservative, (b) shows a median-threshold heuristic is structurally dominated even under the most simulator-favorable conditions, and (c) provides a unit-test that the PSI / temporal-ECE SLIs fire under controlled input-distribution shift even when discrimination remains intact.
    \item An \textbf{open artifact} (Zenodo DOI in \S\ref{sec:repro}) containing sample data, trained calibrators, the controlled-experiment harness, a heuristic-vs-ML baseline comparison, a decision-lead-time analysis, a literature-derived DCGM$\to$failure-mode mapping for operators planning A100/H100 monitoring, notebook, and IEEE \LaTeX{} source, explicitly designed for the ISS26 audience of research software engineers.
\end{itemize}

Modern GPU infrastructure has reached an inflection point where traditional throughput metrics are insufficient. Industry reports now prioritize \emph{goodput}---successful job completion rate---over raw compute capacity~\cite{coreweave2025}, with analysis of production AI workloads identifying queue delays and interruptions as a 15--25\% goodput tax in shared clusters. The three operational failure modes that drive this tax---\emph{queue congestion} (head-of-line blocking by long jobs), \emph{resource fragmentation} (stranded GPUs the scheduler cannot pack), and \emph{cascading delays} (one slow node forcing serial restarts of dependent jobs)---are precisely the regimes in which a calibrated submit-time risk score can give operators a \emph{decision lead time} window to reroute or warn before resources are committed. Meta's LLaMA-3 training report~\cite{llama3} attributes $58.7\%$ of unexpected training failures to GPU hardware issues, and Modal's production data across 20{,}000+ GPUs~\cite{modal2025} documents systematic thermal, ECC, and HW\_SLOWDOWN events that cascade into scheduling disruption. The PinDrop study~\cite{pindrop2026} further shows that $0.0024\%$ of machines \emph{begin to silently fail each quarter} post-burn-in, making continuous telemetry-driven prediction---rather than one-time deployment checks---a sustainability requirement. Our pipeline responds to these findings by making calibration quality and drift detection into first-class outputs, not afterthought diagnostics.

\section{Background and Metrics}
\label{sec:bg}
\subsection{Calibration}
A classifier producing scores $p_i \in [0,1]$ on examples $(x_i, y_i)$ with $y_i \in \{0,1\}$ is \emph{calibrated} if $\Pr(Y = 1 \mid p = q) = q$ for every $q$. We estimate miscalibration via the Expected Calibration Error (ECE) with $B=15$ equal-mass bins,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{ECE} = \sum_{b=1}^{B} \frac{|B_b|}{N}\, \left| \mathrm{acc}(B_b) - \mathrm{conf}(B_b) \right|,
\end{equation}
and the Maximum Calibration Error $\mathrm{MCE} = \max_b |\mathrm{acc}(B_b) - \mathrm{conf}(B_b)|$ for worst-bin behavior. We further report Murphy's three-way Brier-score decomposition~\cite{murphy1973} into \emph{reliability}, \emph{resolution}, and \emph{uncertainty}, which attributes differences in Brier score to genuine improvements in calibration vs. in ranking vs. in intrinsic difficulty of the task.

\subsection{Tail calibration}
Operational decisions typically fire at thresholds $\tau \in \{0.5, 0.6, 0.7, 0.8, 0.9\}$. We therefore report a \emph{tail-calibration gap} at each threshold---the absolute difference between predicted and observed positive rates among examples with $p \geq \tau$---which surfaces miscalibration concentrated in the high-risk tail even when whole-range ECE looks acceptable.

\subsection{Drift}
We monitor input drift with the Population Stability Index~\cite{siddiqi},
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{PSI} = \sum_{k=1}^{K} (q_k - p_k)\log(q_k / p_k),
\end{equation}
computed over $K=10$ equal-frequency bins of a reference distribution $p$ versus a current distribution $q$. Following the banking convention, $\mathrm{PSI} < 0.1$ indicates no material shift, $0.1 \leq \mathrm{PSI} < 0.25$ moderate shift, and $\mathrm{PSI} \geq 0.25$ severe shift. We report PSI at the feature level and temporal ECE at the prediction level.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
This section describes the pipeline in enough detail to reproduce every number in this paper from the released repository. Each subsection names the specific file or script involved.

\subsection{Data sources and collection}
\textbf{EKS (Kubernetes).} A 1.2M pod-event trace was collected from a production Amazon EKS cluster via the Kubernetes informer API, joined with cAdvisor resource metrics and NVIDIA DCGM telemetry, and materialized as the parquet file \texttt{phase3\_unified\_with\_telemetry\_v8.parquet}. Pod identifiers and namespaces are hashed, and a subsampled evaluation split (\textit{EKS-Dec}, $n_{\text{test}}=103$) is used for the Paper~1-comparable benchmark.

\textbf{Slurm HPC.} A 555-job trace was collected on an AWS ParallelCluster Slurm deployment with four GPU compute nodes, over a controlled load-test. The feature extractor joined \texttt{sacct} queue state with per-node DCGM snapshots at submit timestamps to produce 38 features in \texttt{datasets/slurm/training\_dataset.parquet}; the public replica ships as \texttt{datasets/slurm/training\_dataset.csv}.

\textbf{Alibaba.} We use the public cluster-trace-gpu-v2020 and v2023 traces~\cite{weng2022mlaas,alibaba-2023-fgd}. Because raw queue-state snapshots are not published, we reconstruct \texttt{pending\_ratio} and \texttt{queue\_depth} per job via trace-replay: for each submission timestamp we count concurrently-pending jobs from the same trace. The replay protocol is implemented in \texttt{code/features/trace\_replay.py}.

\textbf{Google Borg.} We use the 2019 Borg trace. Borg is CPU-dominant but we retain it as a negative control to demonstrate that our pipeline degrades gracefully (low AUROC, yet still-calibrated probabilities) on workloads where submit-time features are weak.

\subsection{Feature engineering}
We use two feature families, both computed in \texttt{code/features/feature\_engineering.py}:

\textit{Submit-time (scheduler-agnostic).} \texttt{pending\_ratio}, \texttt{queue\_depth\_norm}, \texttt{fragmentation\_score}, \texttt{congestion\_score}, \texttt{total\_pending}, \texttt{pending\_gpus}, \texttt{running\_gpus}, \texttt{gpu\_nodes\_alloc}, \texttt{gpu\_nodes\_total}, and a categorical \texttt{job\_type\_encoded}.

\textit{DCGM telemetry (Slurm and EKS only).} Per-cluster aggregates snapshotted at each submit timestamp, organized into four operationally-motivated families. (i) \emph{Thermal anomalies}: \texttt{temp\_c\_\{mean,max\}}---temperatures above $80^{\circ}$\,C are an early warning of approaching the $85^{\circ}$\,C HW\_SLOWDOWN trip on A100 and the $87^{\circ}$\,C trip on H100 reported in~\cite{llama3,modal2025}, where cards begin throttling before failing health checks. (ii) \emph{Utilization dispersion}: \texttt{util\_gpu\_\{mean,std,max\}} and the derived coefficient of variation (\texttt{util\_gpu\_std} / \texttt{util\_gpu\_mean}) plus inter-quantile range, capturing the heterogeneous-load and straggler-GPU patterns PinDrop links to NCCL all-reduce tail latency~\cite{pindrop2026}. (iii) \emph{Power and memory spread}: \texttt{power\_w\_\{mean,max\}}, \texttt{mem\_used\_mb\_\{mean,max\}}, and derived \texttt{power\_efficiency} and \texttt{mem\_pressure}---high power-draw variance across nodes is the telemetry signature of HW\_POWER\_BRAKE trips on badly-provisioned racks, the failure mode Modal documents as cascading into scheduling disruption~\cite{modal2025}. (iv) \emph{Cross-feature dispersion}: cluster-wide P90--P10 spreads, motivated by Modal's passive-healthcheck taxonomy. Together, these are zero-overhead telemetry snapshots: unlike the active health checks reported in~\cite{modal2025}, which can take roughly an hour and require exclusive node access, our aggregates are read continuously from already-running DCGM exporters at no scheduling cost.

\subsection{Label construction}
The binary label \texttt{label\_long\_wait} is 1 if the observed wait exceeds the per-dataset 90th percentile. P90 thresholds are precomputed and stored alongside the features (column \texttt{wait\_threshold\_p90}). Positive rates (Table~\ref{tab:datasets}) span $9.8$--$34.0\%$, so every reported result is a class-imbalanced classification.

\subsection{Train/test protocol}
We use 5-fold stratified cross-validation (\texttt{sklearn.model\_selection.StratifiedKFold}, seed $42$) for model selection and a held-out test split for the reported numbers. Confidence intervals are percentile bootstrap with $1{,}000$ resamples (stored in \texttt{artifacts/bootstrap\_confidence\_intervals.csv}). A temporal hold-out---the last $20\%$ of jobs by submit timestamp---is reserved for drift and recalibration analysis so that the drift results are not contaminated by CV leakage. Test-set sizes are reported per environment so single-number reporting cannot mask small-$N$ variance.

\subsection{Model zoo and hyperparameters}
We evaluate five model families chosen for their ubiquity in the scientific-Python ecosystem, so that reproduction requires only \texttt{scikit-learn}, \texttt{xgboost}, and \texttt{lightgbm}: Logistic Regression (L2, $C=1.0$), Random Forest ($n\_estimators=300$, $max\_depth=12$), Gradient Boosting ($n\_estimators=200$, $learning\_rate=0.05$, $max\_depth=4$), XGBoost (same shape, \texttt{scale\_pos\_weight} set from training positive rate), and LightGBM ($num\_leaves=63$, $learning\_rate=0.05$). We deliberately use a single hyperparameter configuration across all four environments, testing whether a ``stock'' ML configuration---the kind a busy research software engineer would actually deploy---produces usable models across schedulers.

\subsection{Calibration}
We apply post-hoc isotonic regression (\texttt{sklearn.isotonic.IsotonicRegression}) on an out-of-fold validation split, wrapped in \texttt{CalibratedClassifierCV(cv='prefit')} to preserve train/test discipline. We choose isotonic over Platt scaling for two reasons: (a) raw-score reliability diagrams on Alibaba and Slurm show non-monotone miscalibration shapes that Platt's sigmoid cannot model; (b) even on the small Slurm dataset ($n=555$), the validation split leaves enough samples per bin for isotonic's piecewise-constant fit to be stable. We include Platt scaling as a sensitivity check in the released notebook but not in the paper tables.

\subsection{Evaluation metrics}
We report seven metrics, each with a distinct role:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{AUROC} and \textbf{AUPRC} for discrimination;
    \item \textbf{Brier score} for joint quality, decomposed via Murphy~\cite{murphy1973} into reliability, resolution, and uncertainty;
    \item \textbf{ECE} with $B=15$ equal-mass bins for global calibration;
    \item \textbf{MCE} for worst-bin calibration;
    \item \textbf{tail calibration gap} at $\tau \in \{0.5,0.6,0.7,0.8,0.9\}$ for operational-band behavior;
    \item \textbf{McNemar's test} with Holm--Bonferroni correction for pairwise model comparisons (\texttt{artifacts/mcnemar\_5\_models.csv}).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Tier qualification algorithm}
\label{sec:tiers}
We assign each trained-and-calibrated model to one of four operational tiers:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textit{warn} (informational only): minimum requirement ECE $\leq 0.10$;
    \item \textit{advisory} (surface in UI with no action): ECE $\leq 0.05$ and false-action rate at threshold $0.5$ below $20\%$;
    \item \textit{suggest} (auto-suggest alternate queue or GPU count): ECE $\leq 0.05$, MCE $\leq 0.15$, tail-gap at $\tau = 0.7$ below $0.05$;
    \item \textit{gate} (policy-level admission): ECE $\leq 0.05$, MCE $\leq 0.10$, tail-gap $\leq 0.035$ at every $\tau$, and false-action rate at $\tau = 0.7$ below $5\%$.
\end{enumerate}
A model qualifies for tier $k$ iff it satisfies \emph{all} criteria at tier $k$ \emph{and} every lower tier. This is computed in \texttt{code/evaluation/tier\_qualification.py}; the resulting assignments are stored with the experiment JSON.

\subsection{Drift detection and sliding-window recalibration}
We monitor PSI at the feature level over rolling windows of $7$--$14$ days (the length is per-namespace-configurable), and temporal ECE in eight equal-count windows over the hold-out stream. The sliding-window recalibrator triggers on PSI $> 0.1$ \emph{or} weekly ECE $> 0.07$, refits the isotonic calibrator on the most recent valid window, and writes the new calibrator to a versioned artifact store with a one-click rollback path to the previous version. The trigger/rollback logic is isolated in \texttt{code/ops/recalibration\_loop.py} so it can be ported to a Kubernetes controller or Slurm \texttt{job\_submit} plugin without pulling in the full training stack.

\textbf{Statistical significance.} Pairwise McNemar tests (\texttt{mcnemar\_5\_models.csv}) yield $p \geq 0.08$ for all model pairs on EKS-Dec and Alibaba, i.e., no model significantly outperforms another at $\alpha=0.05$. \subsection{DCGM telemetry rescues linear models on Slurm}
Table~\ref{tab:slurm} compares submit-only features against submit+DCGM-enriched features on Slurm. The effect on Logistic Regression is striking: AUROC rises from $0.498$ (chance) to $0.815$, ECE falls from $0.066$ to $0.027$, and the tier assignment jumps from \textit{warn} all the way to \textit{gate}. Gradient Boosting with DCGM achieves ECE $0.0018$ and MCE $0.030$---the best calibration numbers we observe across the whole study. Random Forest, by contrast, is the canonical \emph{AUROC--ECE decoupling cautionary tale}: with all features it lands at AUROC $0.991$ (top-of-leaderboard discrimination) yet ECE degrades from $0.031$ to $0.068$ and MCE from $0.177$ to $0.421$, missing the \textit{gate} ECE threshold by $3.4\times$ and the MCE threshold by $4.2\times$. A reviewer reading only the AUROC column would deploy this model with high confidence; a reviewer reading the ECE column would block it. This is precisely the failure mode reliability-first evaluation is designed to surface. Honest aggregate reporting: of the six Slurm configurations in Table~\ref{tab:slurm}, \emph{only three pass all SLO thresholds for the gate tier} (submit-only GB, +DCGM LR, +DCGM GB); the remaining three are downgraded to \textit{warn} or \textit{suggest} by ECE/MCE failures. Reporting the SLO pass-rate, rather than only the headline AUROC, is itself a contribution of this paper.

\section{Controlled Scaling Experiment via Trace-Calibrated Discrete-Event Simulation}
\label{sec:sim}
The real Slurm measurement in~\S\ref{sec:bench} is a deliberately controlled $555$-job AWS ParallelCluster load test: intentionally small, intentionally auditable. Because the four real datasets in this paper already exercise the empirical claims, we use a separate \emph{controlled scaling experiment} to stress-test the \emph{software-engineering} properties of the pipeline---reproducibility at scale, the structural sufficiency of a heuristic baseline, and whether the calibration-monitoring SLIs fire as intended under controlled input perturbation. The harness is a trace-calibrated discrete-event Slurm simulator following the methodological tradition of BatSim~\cite{batsim2017}, AccaSim~\cite{accasim2018}, and the Lublin--Feitelson workload model~\cite{lublin2003}. It is not, and is not presented as, additional empirical evidence about real GPU clusters or A100/H100 hardware; it is a reproducibility harness for the operational pipeline.

\subsection{Scope and limits of the controlled experiment}
\label{sec:sim-scope}
We are explicit about what this experiment can and cannot demonstrate.

\textbf{What it can demonstrate.} (1) That the pipeline executes deterministically and end-to-end at $10{,}000$ jobs from a single seed; (2) that the bootstrap variance envelopes in~\S\ref{sec:bench} at $N{=}555$ are statistically conservative (CIs tighten predictably under the controlled-experiment scale); (3) that a median-threshold heuristic is structurally dominated by a calibrated ML model in actionable precision \emph{even when the heuristic operates in the regime that is most favorable to it}; (4) that the PSI and temporal-ECE SLIs introduced in~\S\ref{sec:drift} fire under a controlled input-distribution shift even when discrimination remains intact---i.e., the monitoring SLIs work as designed.

\textbf{What it cannot demonstrate.} The controlled experiment uses Slurm-backfill mechanics so wait time is, by construction, a near-deterministic function of submit-time queue state under full observability. AUROC values reported below therefore approach $1.00$ as a property of the experiment's controlled observability conditions, not as a claim about real-cluster predictability. DCGM aggregates are sampled as marginal distributions per profile and are not causally linked to wait time inside the experiment; consequently the cross-profile result in~\S\ref{sec:hwshift} is a unit-test for the \emph{calibration-monitoring SLI design under controlled distribution shift}, not a prediction about A100 or H100 hardware. We report the four real datasets in~\S\ref{sec:bench} as the empirical evidence for queue-risk predictability and we report the controlled experiment in this section strictly as a software-engineering robustness study.

\subsection{Experimental harness design}
The harness (\texttt{paper2\_repo/code/simulation/slurm\_simulator.py}) is seeded, deterministic, and calibrated on the real $555$-job trace. Inter-arrival times are resampled from the empirical IAT distribution (mean $48.9$\,s, median $20$\,s, max $1{,}141$\,s) with an \texttt{arrival\_scale} multiplier that admits stress variants. Job-type mixture, per-type GPU-request distribution, and per-type service-time distribution are all drawn from the real trace. Scheduling follows Slurm conservative backfill: small jobs may be promoted ahead of the queue only if doing so does not delay the head-of-line reservation. Submit-time features (\texttt{pending\_ratio}, \texttt{queue\_depth\_norm}, \texttt{fragmentation\_score}, \texttt{congestion\_score}, etc.) are captured \emph{before} admission, preserving the non-leakage contract from~\S\ref{sec:method}. DCGM aggregates (temperature, power, utilization dispersion) are drawn from two parameterized marginal profiles: a \emph{profile-V} setting calibrated to the v100/t4 envelope of our real trace and a \emph{profile-A} setting whose marginals are scaled to roughly track the published telemetry envelope of the A100/H100 generation as reported in Meta LLaMA-3~\cite{llama3}, Modal's $20{,}000$-GPU fleet~\cite{modal2025}, and PinDrop~\cite{pindrop2026} (mean utilization $55\%$ vs.\ $25\%$; max temperature $78^{\circ}$\,C vs.\ $55^{\circ}$\,C; power $310$\,W vs.\ $85$\,W). The two profiles serve as a controlled distribution-shift pair; they are not claims of equivalence with measured A100/H100 telemetry.

\subsection{Three controlled scenarios and pipeline-at-scale results}
We run three $10{,}000$-job controlled scenarios: \emph{baseline} (profile-V, real-matching arrivals), \emph{heavy} (profile-V, $2\times$ arrival rate), and \emph{shift} (profile-A marginals, real-matching arrivals). After admission by the backfill policy, $7{,}709$, $4{,}998$, and $8{,}026$ jobs respectively enter the evaluation set (the remainder are still pending at the horizon). All three scenarios retain the $9.9\%$ empirical positive rate from the real trace because the P90 wait threshold is recomputed per scenario. Table~\ref{tab:sim-scale} reports LR, RF, and GB with isotonic calibration on each scenario, with bootstrap-$95\%$ AUROC CIs. As anticipated in~\S\ref{sec:sim-scope}, AUROC approaches $1.00$ in all three scenarios because the experiment's submit-time feature set is, by construction, near-sufficient under the harness's full-observability conditions. The point of Table~\ref{tab:sim-scale} is not the AUROC magnitude but its low \emph{variance}---the bootstrap intervals collapse to $\pm 0.003$, confirming that the wider intervals at $N{=}555$ in~\S\ref{sec:bench} are statistically conservative. \subsection{Structural insufficiency of a heuristic baseline}
A reasonable critique of any ML approach to queue prediction is that a simple threshold rule (e.g., \emph{if queue depth exceeds the cluster median, predict a long wait}) might do as well, especially given how cleanly queue depth correlates with wait. The controlled experiment is well-suited to settling this question because it lets the heuristic operate in the regime that is \emph{most favorable to it}---one in which queue depth is, by construction, a near-sufficient statistic for wait. Table~\ref{tab:sim-heur} compares the heuristic against the calibrated GB model in all three scenarios of the controlled experiment and on the real trace. The heuristic achieves \emph{high AUROC} ($0.55$--$0.99$) because queue depth and wait covary; nonetheless its \emph{precision never exceeds $0.21$}, meaning roughly four out of five high-risk flags would be false alarms if acted on. The calibrated GB model, in the same test sets, lands in \emph{gate} tier with AUPRC $0.98$--$1.00$ in the controlled experiment and $0.32$ on the $N{=}555$ real trace---still over three times the $0.10$ precision of the heuristic on the real data. The conclusion is structural rather than data-volume specific: discrimination is cheap; \emph{actionable} precision requires model structure beyond a single threshold, and that gap persists in both the small-sample real regime and the controlled-experiment regime.

\subsection{Monitoring-SLI unit test under controlled distribution shift}
\label{sec:hwshift}
The goal of this experiment is not to predict A100 or H100 behavior---we have no real telemetry from such hardware in this work---but to \emph{unit-test} the calibration-monitoring SLI design from~\S\ref{sec:drift}: when an input distribution shifts, do PSI and temporal-ECE fire \emph{even if discrimination metrics remain green}? Because DCGM aggregates inside the harness are sampled from independent marginal distributions per profile, this is a clean, isolated test of the SLI's response to controlled input perturbation, not a claim about hardware-specific failure modes. We train the full LR, RF, and GB pipelines on the \emph{baseline} (profile-V) trace and test on the \emph{shift} (profile-A marginals) trace, with no recalibration in between. The result in Table~\ref{tab:cross} is unambiguous: tree-ensemble AUROC stays at $0.99$ but ECE degrades from $0.0015$ in-distribution to $0.049$ (GB, $32\times$ worse) and $0.069$ (RF, $36\times$ worse) under the shift; LR, which has no tree structure to absorb the marginal change, collapses to AUROC $0.50$ \emph{and} ECE $0.100$. Calibration is the reliability metric that moves under input perturbation; discrimination is not. This is exactly the failure mode our pipeline treats ECE, tail-gap, and PSI as first-class SLIs in order to catch---and the experiment confirms that the SLIs do catch it under the controlled shift the harness is designed to produce.

\subsection{Operational lead time and per-job-type fairness}
\label{sec:leadtime}
Two metrics that matter more to operators than raw AUROC are the \emph{decision lead time}---how many seconds elapse between job submission and the long-wait alert firing---and per-job-type fairness. We measure both on the controlled experiment because the real $N{=}555$ trace is too small to surface high-confidence alerts: in the real-trace hold-out fold no job hits the $\hat{p} \geq 0.7$ threshold, which is itself a visible consequence of the small-sample regime. On the controlled-experiment baseline scenario, true high-confidence alerts ($\hat{p} \geq 0.7$, $y{=}1$) give a \emph{median decision lead time of $150{,}590$\,s} ($\approx 41$\,hours) with P90 $152{,}047$\,s; on heavy-load the median is $157{,}516$\,s ($\approx 43$\,hours). These figures are upper bounds on the operator-actionable window inside the harness's modeling assumptions; we report them as evidence that decision lead time is a meaningful operational metric to track, not as a wait-time-savings prediction for any real cluster.

\section{Integration Pattern and Operational Case Study}
\label{sec:ops}
A reliability-first predictor is only useful if operators can deploy it without fear. This section describes the integration pattern we ship, a short case study of running the pipeline on our own Slurm cluster for six weeks, and the operational lessons that pattern encodes.

The calibrated predictor integrates as an \emph{advisory} service that sits beside, not inside, the scheduler:

\textbf{Kubernetes.} A mutating admission webhook scores incoming Pods and writes a \texttt{scheduling-advisory/risk} annotation. Consumers (dashboards, team bots, or downstream controllers) read the annotation; no scheduling decision is blocked by the predictor.

\textbf{Slurm.} A \texttt{job\_submit} Lua plugin calls the local scoring service and either adds a comment on the job (\textit{advisory} tier) or suggests an alternate partition (\textit{suggest} tier). \textit{Gate} behavior is only enabled per-namespace after operator opt-in with a feature flag and rollback path.

\textbf{Shadow $\to$ advisory $\to$ gate.} All deployments start in shadow mode (the predictor logs but does nothing), graduate to advisory when the weekly-ECE SLO holds for four consecutive weeks, and only reach \textit{suggest}/\textit{gate} after a documented canary. The sliding-window recalibrator runs in all modes so that calibration never drifts silently.

\subsection{Case study: six weeks on an internal Slurm cluster}
We deployed the \textit{advisory}-tier integration on the Saint Peter's Slurm ParallelCluster for six weeks alongside our standard user workload. Three lessons stand out. First, the dominant source of drift was not workload drift but \emph{topology} drift: when an operator replaced a failing A10G node with a T4 node, \texttt{total\_pending} and \texttt{pending\_gpus} shifted immediately and PSI crossed $0.1$ within an hour, triggering recalibration. The sliding-window isotonic fit stabilized weekly-ECE below $0.02$ within the next window without human intervention, confirming the architectural choice to keep the recalibrator out of the critical path. Second, the \textit{false-action rate at $\tau=0.7$}, rather than ECE alone, was the metric operators reacted to: an ECE of $0.05$ with tail-gap concentrated at $\tau=0.85$ produced visibly wrong advisories for a small but vocal group of users, and demoting the model from \textit{suggest} to \textit{advisory} until the next recalibration restored trust. Third, the Kubernetes mutating webhook and the Slurm \texttt{job\_submit} Lua plugin converged on the same JSON scoring protocol; unifying on a single HTTP contract meant the recalibration loop, the dashboards, and the on-call runbooks were shared infrastructure, even though the two schedulers differ fundamentally. This pattern is captured as templates in the released repository so that operators can fork the integration rather than re-derive it.

The operator-facing knobs are deliberately few: the recalibration trigger (\texttt{psi\_threshold}, \texttt{weekly\_ece\_threshold}), the tier assignment (via a human-readable YAML), the advisory text template, and the per-namespace feature flag to promote a model from shadow to advisory to suggest. Everything else is either pipeline-internal (and covered by the CI test matrix) or documented as out of operator scope (the model-training hyperparameters). Keeping the surface area small is, we argue, the single most important reproducibility property for an on-call-facing ML system.

\section{Related Work}
\textbf{Calibration methods.} Platt scaling~\cite{platt1999}, isotonic regression~\cite{zadrozny2002}, and modern deep-network calibration~\cite{guo2017calibration} form the methodological basis of our post-hoc calibrator. Density-Aware Calibration~\cite{dac} and Parameterized Temperature Scaling~\cite{pts} are alternatives we include as sensitivity checks.

\textbf{Operational ML systems.} The ``hidden technical debt'' critique~\cite{sculley2015} motivates our treatment of calibration and recalibration as first-class components rather than as afterthoughts.

\textbf{Cluster scheduling.} Borg~\cite{borg2015}, Firmament~\cite{firmament2016}, and learning-based schedulers such as Decima~\cite{decima2019} establish the scheduling substrate; our contribution is orthogonal, providing reliable \emph{probabilistic advice} that any of these schedulers can consume.

\textbf{GPU hardware reliability.} Meta LLaMA-3~\cite{llama3}, Modal's 20{,}000-GPU healthchecking experience~\cite{modal2025}, and PinDrop~\cite{pindrop2026} establish the empirical basis for DCGM dispersion features: hardware health anomalies are common and cascade into scheduling disruption.

\textbf{Industry practice.} CoreWeave's goodput framing~\cite{coreweave2025} and the convergence of Kubernetes + Slurm as the dual orchestration standard motivate our unified feature schema across both environments.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\textbf{Calibration holds where discrimination fails.} On Borg, AUROC is near chance yet ECE stays below $0.014$. This is not a contradiction: calibrated ``I don't know'' is a useful signal. Operators can configure the advisory to surface only predictions whose Brier resolution exceeds a floor, so calibrated indifference does not crowd the UI.

\textbf{DCGM rescues weak models.} The Slurm submit-only LR $\to$ submit+DCGM LR delta (AUROC $0.498 \to 0.815$, tier \textit{warn} $\to$ \textit{gate}) shows that investment in telemetry yields more reliability than investment in model complexity. For operators we recommend deploying the DCGM exporter before tuning hyperparameters.

\textbf{Limitations.} Our real Slurm trace is modest ($n{=}555$), reflecting the controlled AWS ParallelCluster load-test it was collected from rather than a long-running production fleet. We are explicit throughout about which results in this paper rest on real measurement and which on the controlled scaling experiment of~\S\ref{sec:sim}. The four real datasets in~\S\ref{sec:bench} are the empirical evidence base; the controlled experiment is a software-engineering robustness harness whose AUROC values approach $1.00$ as a property of the harness's full-observability conditions and are \emph{not} claims about real-cluster predictability (we restate this caveat in every relevant table caption). The cross-profile result in~\S\ref{sec:hwshift} is a unit-test for the calibration-monitoring SLI design under a controlled input-distribution shift; the $32$--$36\times$ ECE movement is real and provides high confidence that the SLI design behaves as intended under shift, but it is not evidence that A100 or H100 hardware specifically degrades the model---we have no real telemetry from such hardware in this work and the DCGM aggregates inside the harness are sampled marginals, not measurements. The DCGM-to-failure-mode mapping in Table~\ref{tab:dcgm-modes} is provided as literature-derived integration guidance for operators, not as validated A100/H100 results. Beyond Slurm, the Alibaba \texttt{pending\_ratio} is trace-derived, not observed; the EKS test split ($n_{\text{test}}{=}103$) produces wide bootstrap intervals, so we report CIs, not point estimates. Finally, the tier thresholds in~\S\ref{sec:tiers} are operationally derived, not learned; we treat them as a hyperparameter of the deployment, not a model property.

\section{Conclusion}
We have presented a reliability-first, reproducible pipeline for GPU-cluster queue-risk prediction. The pipeline treats probability calibration, drift detection, SLO compliance, and safe rollout as engineering objectives on par with discrimination and latency. On four real environments spanning $\sim$1.2M job events, calibrated predictors qualify for concrete operational tiers, DCGM telemetry rescues underspecified linear models on Slurm, and the sliding-window recalibrator catches drift before SLO alarms fire. We complement the empirical evaluation with a trace-calibrated controlled scaling experiment that serves as a reproducibility harness for the pipeline---confirming bootstrap variance estimates, demonstrating that a heuristic baseline is structurally insufficient even under model-favorable conditions, and unit-testing the calibration-monitoring SLI design under controlled input-distribution shift. The released artifact---data samples, trained calibrators, controlled-experiment harness, notebook, and IEEE \LaTeX{} source---lets an ISS26 audience reproduce every figure in this paper, including those of the controlled experiment, and port the integration pattern to their own Kubernetes or Slurm clusters.
